A Little Bit About Me

I'll start with a confession: I don’t know everything. But honestly, that’s what makes the journey interesting. What I do know is how to learn quickly, connect ideas across fields, and turn them into something useful.

I began with a Bachelor of Business Administration and later a post-graduate degree in Data Science for Marketing, which opened the door to the STEM path I’m now fully pursuing with a Master’s in Data Science. But my foundation goes back further—high school in Science and Technology gave me a taste of biology, chemistry, and especially the beauty of networks and genetics. Those interests still echo today, whether I’m exploring network science in my master’s, experimenting with genetic algorithms, or even applying chemistry principles in the kitchen when I cook.

Professionally, I’ve had the chance to bridge business and science in very different settings. I spent a year at one of Portugal’s largest wine companies, in the International Trade department, collaborating with markets such as Spain, France, China, and across Latin America. To sell wine globally, I had to understand not only the logistics and commercial side but also the process and culture behind it. Being based in the vineyards meant daily contact with oenology, soil, and production—an experience that connected the business of wine with the science that makes it possible. Later, at NOS Technology, Portugal’s leading telecommunications provider, I shifted focus to data, analyzing mobile network performance by studying cell throughput versus customer satisfaction. These experiences taught me how valuable it is to move between worlds, and how data connects them all.

Outside of work, I trained in ballet for 11 years and later in martial arts. Different? Yes. But together they shaped my mindset: discipline, adaptability, and the ability to use whatever I have to my advantage.

For me, the red thread is curiosity. As Charles Eames said, Eventually everything connects—people, ideas, objects, and I share that belief. Being a generalist at heart, I see value in interdisciplinary knowledge as a way to spark new ideas—echoing Steve Jobs’s reminder that creativity is just connecting things.

So no, I don’t know everything. But I do know how to explore, to connect, and to create—and I bring that mindset to every project and team I join.

Predicting NY Workers’ Compensation Claims Outcomes

Ranked among the top projects (18.10/20), we developed machine learning models for the New York Workers’ Compensation Board to predict insurance claim outcomes from a complex, imbalanced dataset. We built multiclass models to classify injury types and binary classifiers to predict claim agreements. Since only a sampled dataset was available due to hardware limits, less-represented classes were especially hard to model. With the professor’s own F1 score of ~0.5 as a benchmark, our best models achieved F1 scores of 0.46 with 0.77 accuracy (multiclass) and 0.63 with 0.90 accuracy (binary), showing acceptable predictive performance within these constraints.
